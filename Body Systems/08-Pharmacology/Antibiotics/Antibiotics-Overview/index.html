    Antibiotics Overview

    Last updated: 2026 · ~50 min read

    Quick Reference

    Gram+ ONLY Vancomycin, Linezolid, Daptomycin, Clindamycin
    Gram− ONLY Aztreonam
    Anaerobe coverage Clindamycin (above diaphragm), Metronidazole (below), Beta-lac/BLI combos, Moxifloxacin, Meropenem
    Broad G+/G− 3rd/4th gen cephalosporins, Beta-lac/BLI combos, Carbapenems, Moxifloxacin
    MRSA coverage Vancomycin, Linezolid, Daptomycin, Ceftaroline, Clindamycin, Tetracyclines, TMP-SMX
    Pseudomonas Pip/Tazo, Ceftazidime, Cefepime, Aztreonam, Cipro ± , Meropenem, Imipenem
    VRE Daptomycin, Linezolid, Ampicillin

    Clinical Pearl

    If a GNR (esp. E. coli, Klebsiella, Proteus) is resistant to ceftriaxone → suspect ESBL. Avoid all beta-lactams.

    I. Beta-Lactams

    Classes: PCNs, Cephalosporins, Carbapenems, Monobactam (Aztreonam)

    Mechanism: Bind PBPs in cell membrane → inhibit cell wall crosslinking → bactericidal

    Side effects: Hypersensitivity/anaphylaxis, rashes, bone marrow suppression, interstitial nephritis, GI (C. diff), seizures (high dose + renal failure)

    Key rule

    No beta-lactam covers MRSA (except Ceftaroline) or atypical intracellular organisms (Legionella, Mycoplasma, Chlamydia).

    Penicillin Allergy

    Situation Action
    Reported PCN allergy ~85–90% will actually tolerate PCN
    Cross-reactivity with cephalosporins ~2% (in those with positive skin test)
    Cross-reactivity with carbapenems <1%
    PCN + Aztreonam No cross-reactivity (except Aztreonam ↔ Ceftazidime — identical side chain)

    A. Penicillins

    1. Penicillin G (IV) / V (PO)

    • Spectrum: Streptococci, minority of Staph, some Enterococcus, oral anaerobes, Syphilis
    • DOC for Group A Strep (universally PCN-sensitive)
    • Used for: Strep throat, Syphilis, bacteremia/endocarditis from susceptible organisms

    2. Aminopenicillins — Ampicillin (IV), Amoxicillin (PO)

    • Spectrum: Strep, Enterococcus, Listeria — NOT MSSA; limited GN (no Klebsiella, Moraxella, SPICE-A)
    • DOC for Enterococcal infections if susceptible (E. faecalis usually yes, E. faecium usually not)
    • Amoxicillin = best-absorbed beta-lactam (75–90% bioavailability)

    3. Anti-Staphylococcal PCNs — Nafcillin/Oxacillin (IV), Dicloxacillin (PO)

    • Spectrum: MSSA, Strep — no MRSA
    • DOC for MSSA infections
    • For serious MSSA infections (bacteremia, osteomyelitis, endocarditis) → must be IV for entire course

    4. Pip/Tazo (Zosyn) — IV

    • Broad: G+, G− including Pseudomonas, anaerobes
    • Does NOT cover: MRSA, most VRE, Atypicals, ESBLs
    • PNA/Pseudomonas dosing: 4.5 g q6h (vs 3.375 g q6h for other indications)

    B. Cephalosporins

    General Rules

    • No cephalosporin covers Enterococcus (except Ceftaroline)
    • Only Ceftazidime and Cefepime cover Pseudomonas
    • Only Cefoxitin and Cefotetan have good anaerobic coverage
    Generation Drug Coverage highlights
    1st Cefazolin (IV), Cephalexin (PO) MSSA, Strep, minor GN (PEK)
    2nd Cefuroxime + H. flu, Neisseria
    2nd (cephamycin) Cefoxitin, Cefotetan Anaerobes, GN — no Pseudo, weak GP
    3rd Ceftriaxone Excellent GN, good GP — no Pseudo, no anaerobes
    3rd Ceftazidime GN only including Pseudomonas — virtually no GP
    4th Cefepime Broad GP + GN including Pseudomonas, weak anaerobes
    5th Ceftaroline MRSA + Strep + GN (like Ceftriaxone) — no Pseudo

    SPICE-A organisms

    Serratia, Pseudomonas/Providencia, Indole-positive Proteus, Citrobacter, Enterobacter, Acinetobacter — have inducible AmpC beta-lactamases. Avoid lower-gen cephalosporins for serious infections. Use Cefepime, Pip/Tazo, or Carbapenem.

    C. Carbapenems

    Drugs: Imipenem, Meropenem, Ertapenem, Doripenem (all IV)

    • Broadest spectrum — G+, G− including Pseudomonas and ESBL, anaerobes
    • Does NOT cover: MRSA, most VRE, Atypicals, Stenotrophomonas
    • Ertapenem ≠ Pseudomonas — convenient once-daily dosing, still covers ESBL

    D. Monobactam — Aztreonam

    • Spectrum: Aerobic gram-negatives only (similar to Ceftazidime)
    • No cross-reactivity with PCN allergy ← key advantage
    • Significant Pseudomonas resistance at many institutions → empirically double-cover

    II. Protein Synthesis Inhibitors

    1. Macrolides (50S)

    • Azithromycin = DOC for most atypical infections
    • ~25% Strep pneumo resistance → combine with Ceftriaxone for hospitalized CAP
    • Side effects: QT prolongation, GI

    2. Tetracyclines — Doxycycline (30S)

    • Covers: Atypicals, MRSA (skin), Rickettsia, Lyme, Tularemia, Anthrax
    • DOC for early Lyme disease
    • Good choice for MRSA skin infections (but poor strep coverage → add beta-lactam)

    3. Clindamycin (50S)

    • Covers: Anaerobes + Gram+ cocci (Strep, Staph including ~50% CA-MRSA) — NOT Enterococcus
    • Antitoxin effect in TSS / necrotizing fasciitis (Group A Strep)
    • Always check D-test before using for MRSA
    • Does NOT penetrate CSF
    • Highest rate of C. diff (~10%) of all antibiotics

    4. Aminoglycosides (30S)

    • Covers: Aerobic gram-negatives including Pseudomonas — no GP, no anaerobes
    • Main use: serious GN infections + synergy for endocarditis (Enterococcal > Strep > Staph)
    • Side effects: Nephrotoxicity (reversible), ototoxicity (irreversible)
    • Concentration-dependent killing — high peak matters more than time above MIC

    III. Fluoroquinolones (DNA Gyrase Inhibitors)

    All FQs: excellent bioavailability (use PO whenever possible), atypical coverage, QT prolongation risk

    Drug Key features
    Ciprofloxacin Best GN coverage, virtually no GP — UTI, Pseudomonas double-coverage
    Levofloxacin "Respiratory FQ" — excellent Strep pneumo, CAP monotherapy ok
    Moxifloxacin Best anaerobes/GP — no urinary or Pseudomonas activity

    TB caution

    FQs cover TB (Moxi > Levo > Cipro). If you suspect TB, do NOT use FQs — monotherapy causes resistance.

    IV. TMP-SMX (Bactrim)

    • Covers: Most CA-MRSA, PCP, Nocardia, Toxoplasma, Listeria, Stenotrophomonas, UTI pathogens
    • Best oral option for CA-MRSA skin infections (weak strep coverage → add Cephalexin)
    • ~100% PO bioavailability
    • Side effects: Rash, hyperkalemia (esp. high dose/CKD), pseudo-Cr elevation (~20%), bone marrow suppression

    V. Super Gram-Positive Antibiotics

    Vancomycin

    • Gold standard for MRSA
    • NOT as effective as Nafcillin for MSSA
    • PO form not absorbed → used for severe C. diff
    • Goal trough: 15–20 for serious/invasive MRSA; 20 for CNS infections
    • Avoid if MRSA MIC ≥ 2 (increased treatment failure)

    Linezolid

    • Covers all GP including MRSA and VRE
    • 100% PO bioavailability
    • May be superior to Vancomycin for MRSA pneumonia (better lung penetration)
    • Side effects: Thrombocytopenia (check CBC weekly if >1 week use), serotonin syndrome with SSRIs, lactic acidosis (long-term)

    Daptomycin

    • Rapidly cidal, covers MRSA and VRE
    • CANNOT use for pneumonia — inactivated by pulmonary surfactant
    • Side effect: Myopathy/rhabdomyolysis — check baseline + weekly CK, stop statins
    • Resistance commonly emerges with long-term use

    VI. Anti-Pseudomonal Options

    For serious Pseudomonas infections → empirically double-cover until susceptibilities back:

    1. Pip/Tazo (Zosyn)
    2. Ceftazidime, Cefepime
    3. Carbapenems (not Ertapenem)
    4. Aztreonam (high resistance rates — use only if PCN-allergic)
    5. Ciprofloxacin (~70% coverage) > Levofloxacin (~65%)
    6. Aminoglycosides — do NOT use as monotherapy for serious infections

    VII. Anaerobe Coverage

    • Above diaphragm: Clindamycin (covers Peptostreptococcus, microaerophilic Strep)
    • Below diaphragm: Metronidazole (excellent Bacteroides coverage)

    Avoid for serious intraabdominal infections

    Clindamycin, Moxifloxacin, Cefotetan/Cefoxitin — high Bacteroides resistance (up to 40%)

    VIII. Vanc/Zosyn — What It Misses

    Good empiric combo, but watch for:

    1. Atypicals (Legionella, Mycoplasma, Chlamydia) — not adequate for CAP
    2. VRE — suspect if prior vancomycin exposure
    3. ESBL — suspect if recent hospitalization + broad-spectrum abx
    4. Fungal infections — esp. with TPN, central lines, immunosuppression
    5. C. difficile
    6. Stenotrophomonas — esp. with prior carbapenem use
    7. TB / Mycobacteria
    8. Viral infections (influenza, HSV, CMV)
    9. Source control — abx won't cure undrained abscess, infected line, empyema

    Step up from Vanc/Zosyn

    → Linezolid/Meropenem: gains VRE and ESBL coverage, possibly better MRSA PNA coverage
