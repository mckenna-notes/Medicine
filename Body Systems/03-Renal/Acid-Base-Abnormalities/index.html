    Acid-Base Abnormalities

    Last updated: Mar 21, 2026

    Quick Reference

    Pearls

    Learn the compensation equations: the body will not over compensate — it is an additional underlying process.

    Clinical Evaluation of Metabolic Acidosis

    1. Determine Anion Gap
    2. Determine Winter's (underlying additional respiratory derangement)
    3. Determine Delta-Delta (ΔAG / ΔBicarb)

    (Anion Gap − 12) / (Bicarb − 24)

    Result Interpretation
    ΔAG ≈ ΔBicarb No other process. Isolated pure anion gap metabolic acidosis.
    ΔAG >> ΔBicarb Second process increasing bicarbonate → AGMA + Metabolic Alkalosis
    ΔAG << ΔBicarb Second process decreasing bicarbonate → AGMA + NAGMA

    NAGMA → consider ordering urine electrolytes (Na, K, Cl)

    Urine Anion Gap = (UNa + UK) − UCl

    • Positive gap (>1): RTA
    • Negative gap (<1): GI loss, IV Fluid

    Winter's Formulas

    Bootleg Winter's

    pCO₂ should ≈ last 2 digits of pH if respiratory compensation is adequate.

    • e.g. pH 7.15 → pCO₂ should be ~15
    • If pCO₂ is higher → suspect additional respiratory acidosis
    • If pCO₂ is lower → suspect additional respiratory alkalosis

    Simplified Winter's

    Predicted PaCO₂ = HCO₃⁻ + 15

    Works for PaCO₂ between 10–40.

    Real Winter's

    Predicted PaCO₂ = 1.5 × HCO₃⁻ + 8 (±2)

    Determining Prior Bicarb

    HCO₃(before) = HCO₃(now) + (AG(now) − AG(normal))

    Can help identify underlying metabolic derangements prior to the current acute disorder.

    Patho / Physiology

    Bicarbonate Buffer System (Le Chatelier's Principle)

    CO₂ + H₂O ⇌ H₂CO₃ ⇌ H⁺ + HCO₃⁻

    • Metabolic acidosis (↑ H⁺) → left shift → ↑ CO₂ → compensatory hyperventilation
    • Metabolic alkalosis (↑ HCO₃⁻) → right shift → ↓ CO₂ → compensatory hypoventilation + urine bicarb excretion

    pH = balance of CO₂, H⁺, and HCO₃⁻

    Mediator Primary Organ
    CO₂ Lungs (ventilation)
    HCO₃⁻ Kidneys (urine)
    H⁺ GI (stomach acid) and kidneys (urine)

    Acids and Bases in Blood

    When a Brønsted-Lowry acid (e.g., lactic acid, ketoacids) is added to plasma, it dissociates into H⁺ and its conjugate base (an anion, such as lactate⁻ or β-hydroxybutyrate⁻). The H⁺ is buffered primarily by HCO₃⁻, which is consumed in the process → decrease in serum bicarbonate. The conjugate base remains in serum, increasing unmeasured anions → raises the calculated anion gap ([Na⁺] – ([Cl⁻] + [HCO₃⁻])).

    The decrease in serum bicarbonate is directly matched by the accumulation of unmeasured anions, maintaining electroneutrality.

    Compensation

    When the underlying process (respiratory or metabolic) drives pH outside the normal range, the alternate system "compensates."

    • Metabolic acidosis → compensatory respiratory alkalosis

    Lung Compensatory Mechanisms:

    • Hyperventilation → expel CO₂ → ↑ pH (compensatory respiratory alkalosis)
    • Hypoventilation → retain CO₂ → ↓ pH (compensatory respiratory acidosis)

    Kidney Compensatory Mechanisms:

    • Respiratory acidosis → retain HCO₃⁻ → ↑ pH (compensatory metabolic alkalosis)
    • Respiratory alkalosis → excrete HCO₃⁻ → ↓ pH (compensatory metabolic acidosis)

    Anion Gap Pearl

    The mean anion gap is 8–12 mEq/L (SD ±2 mEq/L). An anion gap of 20 is ~4–5 SD above the mean — 99.999% chance of AGMA. No need to wait for blood gases to diagnose.

    Primary Metabolic Acid-Base Derangements

    Anion Gap Metabolic Acidosis (AGMA)

    AGMA occurs when accumulation of acids leads to ↓ pH via ↑ H⁺ and ↑ unmeasured serum anions (A⁻).

    Weak Acid: HA ⇌ H⁺ + A⁻

    Simple DDx: KULTS

    Letter Cause
    K Ketones
    U Uremia
    L Lactate
    T Toxic ingestion (toxic alcohols, APAP, etc.)
    S Salicylates

    Osmolar Gap Screening

    AGMA → measure serum Osm → calculate Osm gap. Easily screens for any alcohol intoxication.

    Common Osmolar Gap AGMA Culprits:

    Substance Source
    Methanol Windshield wiper fluid, paint, paint thinners, wood stains
    Ethylene glycol Antifreeze, engine coolants, industrial solvents
    Propylene glycol Diluent in parenteral meds: diazepam, lorazepam, phenobarbital, phenytoin, nitroglycerin
    Diethylene glycol Industrial solvent
    Isopropyl alcohol Hand sanitizer, rubbing alcohol
    Ethanol Good ol' fashioned alcohol

    Full DDx: GOLDMARK

    Letter Cause Mechanism
    G Glycols (ethanol, ethylene glycol) Consume Krebs cycle intermediaries → organic acid buildup
    O Oxoproline (Acetaminophen) Glutathione depletion → pyroglutamic acid buildup
    L L-Lactic acidosis Type A (hypoperfusion), Type B (catecholamine/medication driven)
    D D-Lactic acidosis Abnormal bacterial fermentation in GI tract (rare; short bowel syndrome)
    M Methanol Consumes Krebs enzymes → formic acid buildup
    A Aspirin (Salicylates) Disrupts Krebs cycle, decouples mitochondria → organic acids + lactic acid; also directly stimulates respiratory centers (typically AGMA + resp. alkalosis)
    R Renal failure (Uremia) GFR <15 → retention of phosphites, sulfites, nitrites
    K Ketoacidosis (DKA) Lactic acid + ketones

    MUDPILES adds:

    • Iron — multifactorial; fluid loss, cardiogenic shock, hepatic failure → lactic acidosis
    • Isoniazid — inhibits lactate→pyruvate conversion via vitamin B6 depletion

    Osmolar Gap Clinical Presentations:

    Substance Products Clinical Features
    Methanol Formic acid AMS, blurry vision, dilated pupils, papilledema
    Ethylene glycol Oxalic acid AMS, CN palsies, flank pain, hematuria, hypocalcemia with tetany, calcium oxalate crystals (envelope-shaped), AKI
    Propylene glycol Lactic acid AKI, liver injury
    Diethylene glycol Diglycolic acid AKI, N/V, hepatitis, pancreatitis, neuropathy, AMS, elevated lactate
    Isopropyl alcohol Acetone AMS, fruity breath, pancreatitis, elevated lactate with normal/mildly elevated AG
    Ethanol Acetaldehyde Ketoacidosis, lactic acidosis, metabolic alkalosis (vomiting)

    Lactate Reference:

    Level Significance
    0.5–2 mM Normal
    2–4 mM Mild/moderately elevated
    >4 mM Unequivocally elevated — concerning for occult shock or serious illness

    Non-Anion Gap Metabolic Acidosis (NAGMA)

    Caused by GI or renal loss of HCO₃⁻ (removal of base rather than addition of acid). Results in equivalent accumulation of serum chloride.

    Simple DDx: HARD

    Letter Cause Mechanism
    H Hyperchloremia Normal saline boluses → ↑ urinary chloride → forces urinary HCO₃⁻ excretion (beta-intercalated cell exchanges HCO₃⁻ for Cl⁻)
    A Acetazolamide Inhibits HCO₃⁻ reabsorption in the proximal tubule
    R Renal Tubular Acidosis Kidney excretes excess HCO₃⁻ when it shouldn't — can be genetic or acquired (e.g., tacrolimus)
    D Diarrhea GI loss of bicarb (pancreas!)

    Full DDx — Non-RTA causes:

    • NaCl, KCl, CaCl₂ administration
    • Plasma exchange with 4% albumin
    • TPN with inadequate acetate
    • Diarrhea (especially secretory), high-output fistulas, pancreatic/biliary drainage
    • Cholestyramine or sevelamer hydrochloride
    • Moderate renal insufficiency (GFR ~20–40 mL/min — inadequate ammoniagenesis)
    • Resolving DKA
    • Ureteroileostomy or ureterosigmoidostomy

    Low Anion Gap (<8)

    Causes:

    Increased Chloride:

    • Hypertriglyceridemia
    • Bromide
    • Iodide

    Decreased Unmeasured Anions:

    • ↓ Albumin
    • ↓ Phosphorus

    Increased Unmeasured Cations:

    • Hyperkalemia
    • Hypercalcemia
    • Hypermagnesemia
    • Lithium
    • Cationic paraproteins (IgG, etc.)

    Metabolic Alkalosis

    Most commonly caused by loss of chloride → retention of bicarbonate. The kidney retains HCO₃⁻ to maintain serum electroneutrality (partly via beta-intercalated cells).

    Three Basic Mechanisms:

    Mechanism Example How
    Urine loss Loop diuretics Inhibit Na/K/2Cl transporter in ascending Loop of Henle → massive Cl⁻ loss → kidney retains HCO₃⁻
    GI loss Vomiting Loss of Cl⁻ from gastric juices (alkaline tide)
    Oral alkalinization Antacid overconsumption Patient ate an entire bottle of Tums

    Myth

    Contraction alkalosis is a lie that we have been told and have been passing down to future generations for far too long.

    DDx:

    Saline Responsive (hypochloremia):

    • True volume depletion
    • NG suction / nausea / vomiting
    • Diuretic use

    Saline Refractory:

    • Hypokalemia
    • Milk-Alkali Syndrome
    • Mineralocorticoid excess states
    • Bartter Syndrome
    • Gitelman Syndrome

    Primary Respiratory Acid-Base Derangements

    Respiratory Acidosis

    Info

    Respiratory acidosis is caused by hypoventilation. Period.

    Hypoventilation has many causes (pneumonia, excess sedation, pneumothorax, etc.) but the clinical presentation will usually reveal those details.

    Respiratory Alkalosis

    Info

    Respiratory alkalosis is caused by hyperventilation. Period.

    Most commonly caused by anxiety or stress. Some pathologies can also drive hyperventilation (e.g., salicylate toxicity).
