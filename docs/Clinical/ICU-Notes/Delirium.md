# Delirium

---

## Definitions

**Delirium (DSM-5 Characteristics):**

- Disturbance in awareness and attention
    - Reduced ability to direct, focus, sustain, and shift attention
- Develops over a **short period of time (hours–days)**, represents a change from baseline, and tends to fluctuate throughout the day
- An additional disturbance in cognition is present
    - Memory, disorientation, language, visuospatial ability, or perception
- Caused by: medical condition, intoxication, substance withdrawal, or medication side effects
- Not better explained by another neurocognitive disorder

**Subtypes:**

| Subtype | Features |
|---|---|
| **Hypoactive** | Somnolence and psychomotor retardation |
| **Hyperactive** | Agitation and increased psychomotor activity |
| **Mixed** | Fluctuations between the two |

**Special Contexts:**

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Postoperative Delirium** | Delirium that presents in the post-op period |
| **Emergence Delirium** | Delirium in the OR immediately after extubation |
| **ICU Delirium** | Delirium that presents during an ICU stay |
| **Sundowning** | Behavioral deterioration during evening hours; most common with preexisting dementia |

---

## Risk Factors

**Patient Factors:**

- Elderly
- Preexisting dementia — *strongest RF in the elderly*
- Malnutrition
- ETOH abuse
- Medical comorbidities
- Psych meds
- Type of surgery: 35% after vascular surgery; 40–60% after hip replacement

**Precipitating Factors:**

- Severe illness
- Infection
- Medications
- Hypoxia
- Electrolyte abnormalities
- Pain
- Dehydration

**ICU-Specific Factors:**

- Bladder catheter use
- Sensory or functional impairment
- Physical restraints
- Sleep deprivation
- Unfamiliar environment

---

## Features and Complications

**Common Associated Features:**

- Psychomotor behavioral disturbances: hypoactivity or hyperactivity with increased sympathetic activity
- Sleep impairment
- Variable emotional disturbances: fear, depression, euphoria, perplexity

**Complications:**

- Delirium-induced persistent cognitive dysfunction can last months–years
- Can accelerate cognitive decline of existing dementia
- Increased morbidity and mortality:

| Timeline | Mortality |
|---|---|
| 1-month after single-day delirium | 14.5% |
| 1-month after ≥3 days of delirium | 39% |
| 6-month mortality | Increased 3.2-fold |
| 12-month mortality | Increases 10% per day of delirium |

- Increased length of stay
- Increased rate of discharge to skilled nursing facility
- Increased rate of readmission
- Increased cost

## Evaluation

### CAM-ICU (Confusion Assessment Method for ICU)

Initial evaluation to assess for delirium. High sensitivity and specificity. Can be used in **ventilated patients**.

**Features:**

1. Acute onset and fluctuating course
2. Inattention
3. Disorganized thinking
4. Altered level of consciousness

**Delirium = Features 1 AND 2 + Either 3 OR 4**

*For evaluation of the agitated patient, see [Agitation](Agitation.md).*

## Prevention and Treatment

**Main Principles:**

- Treat underlying medical conditions
- Appropriate pain control with **non-opioid** medications
- **Avoid opioids and benzodiazepines**
- Frequent reorientation
- Allow regular visits from family and friends for cognitive stimulation
- Support physiologic sleep patterns:
    - Avoid unnecessary nighttime interruptions and reduce nighttime noises
    - Avoid excessive daytime sleeping
    - Provide clocks and keep lights on with windows open during the day
- **Avoid physical restraints** — can worsen delirium
- Early mobilization with physical therapy
- Ensure vision and hearing aids are accessible
- May require use of a bedside sitter

**Management of Agitation:**

- Initial nonpharmacologic attempts are preferred
- May require sedation if violent or a danger to self/others
- *See [Agitation](Agitation.md)*
