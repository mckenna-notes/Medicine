# Breathing Support

*Oxygen Delivery, Consumption, and Extraction Ratio*

---

## Definitions

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **DO₂** | Oxygen delivery |
| **VO₂** | Oxygen consumption |
| **O₂ER** | Oxygen extraction ratio |
| **CaO₂** | Arterial O₂ content |
| **CvO₂** | Venous oxygen content |
| **CO** | Cardiac output |

---

## Oxygen Delivery (DO₂)

**DO₂ = CaO₂ × CO**

### Cardiac Output (CO)

**CO = HR × SV**

**Factors that Increase Stroke Volume:**

- Increased preload (end-diastolic volume)
- Increased contractility
- Decreased afterload / decreased SVR

---

### Arterial Oxygen Content (CaO₂)

Arterial oxygen content is a function of three variables:

| Variable | Source | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Hemoglobin (Hgb)** | CBC | Most clinically significant determinant of CaO₂ |
| **SaO₂** | Pulse ox (SpO₂) | Same impact as Hgb, but generally less variance |
| **PaO₂** | ABG | Least important — only contributes 1–2% |

**Oxygen carrying capacity of Hgb** = amount of O₂ (in mL) bound per gram of Hgb = constant **1.34**

Oxygen partially dissolves in plasma, calculated as: **0.003 × PaO₂**

**CaO₂ = (Hgb × SaO₂ × 1.34) + (PaO₂ × 0.003)**

**DO₂ = CO × (Hgb × SaO₂ × 1.34 + PaO₂ × 0.003)**

---

## Oxy-Hgb Dissociation Curve

Compares partial pressure of oxygen (PaO₂) to percent oxygen saturation of hemoglobin (SaO₂).

| Shift | Effect | Causes |
|---|---|---|
| **Left shift** | ↑ Affinity → O₂ binding | ↓ CO₂, ↓ Temp, ↓ H⁺ (↑ pH), ↓ 2,3-DPG, CO exposure, high altitude |
| **Right shift** | ↓ Affinity → O₂ unloading | ↑ CO₂, ↑ Temp, ↑ H⁺ (↓ pH), ↑ 2,3-DPG, hypoxia |

**Special Notes:**

- Carbon monoxide increases O₂ affinity at other binding sites → left shift
- Hypoxia further decreases O₂ affinity due to 2,3-DPG having increased affinity for deoxygenated Hgb
- Fetal Hgb has low affinity for 2,3-DPG → overall higher affinity for O₂

## Evaluating Oxygenation

### Arterial Blood Gas (ABG)

- Measures and reports **PaO₂** (used in P:F ratio, etc.)
- May also measure SaO₂

**ABG Challenges:**

- Expensive, painful, and time consuming
- Blood loss
- May be contaminated with venous blood
- Although a better measure of lung function, pulse ox better measures **systemic delivery**
- Often misinterpreted — SpO₂ of 88% correlates with PaO₂ of ~55 mmHg

---

### Pulse Oximeter (Pulse Ox)

- Detects **SpO₂** — aspect of respiration, not ventilation

**Pulse Ox Challenges:**

- May have a **bias of +2% in dark skin** — oxygen saturation may be overestimated by ~2%, leading to increased rates of unrecognized hypoxemia
- Requires pulse detection in peripheral circulation — may be abnormal in shock or hypothermia
- Generally not accurate once below 70%
- **Falsely elevated in carbon monoxide poisoning**

---

### ABG vs. Pulse Ox

Pulse oximetry (SpO₂) is generally superior to ABG (PaO₂) in measuring oxygenation.

**When ABG is preferred:**

- If pulse oximeter waveform is unreliable (see "plethysmographic waveform")
- For P/F ratio calculation in ARDS
- Diagnosis of methemoglobinemia

---

### Venous Blood Gas (VBG)

**Sources:**

- Peripheral — from peripheral veins
- Central venous — from a central line
- Mixed venous — from a pulmonary artery catheter

**Benefits over ABG:**

- Easier to obtain, less painful
- Can be obtained while sampling other labs
- Lower risk of complications (hematoma, dissection, thrombosis)

**Challenges:**

- ABG is still the gold standard for evaluating oxygenation, ventilation, and acid-base status
- VBG is generally less well validated than ABG
- VBG and ABG may have worse agreement in severe circulatory failure / hypotension
- VBG may correlate better for pH and pCO₂ than for PaO₂ or SaO₂

## General Target Values

| Parameter | Standard | COPD / ARDS |
|---|---|---|
| SaO₂ / SpO₂ | >92% | 88% |
| PaO₂ | >60 mmHg | 55 mmHg |
| ScvO₂ | >70% | — |

---

## Hypoxic Definitions

- **Hypoxemia:** Low oxygen content in blood
- **Hypoxia:** Low oxygen content in tissues
