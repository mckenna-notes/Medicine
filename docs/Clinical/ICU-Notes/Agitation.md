# Agitation

*The Agitated Patient*

---

## Evaluation

### Interview Safety

!!! danger "Safety First"
    Safety of staff is paramount. Call security if necessary.

- Patients should always be **disarmed** before any interaction
- No potentially dangerous objects should be available
- Do **not** let the patient sit between the physician and the exit

### Risk Factors for Violence

- History of violence
- Male gender
- Alcohol / drug abuse

### Signs of Impending Violence

**Provocative Behavior:**

- Angry demeanor
- Loud, aggressive speech
- Tense posturing
- Frequently changing body position / pacing
- Aggressive acts (pounding wall, throwing objects)
- Verbal threats

### Assess for Medical Causes that May Be Contributing

- Hypoxia
- Inadequate pain control
- Hypoglycemia
- Infection
- Drug overdose
- Intracranial hemorrhage

---

## Management

### General Approach

- Manage delirium as noted — *see [Delirium](Delirium.md)*
- **Initial nonpharmacologic attempts are preferred**, unless violent or a danger to self/others
- **Immediate sedation:** start with a rapid-onset antipsychotic or benzodiazepine
    - Avoid opioids and benzodiazepines in the setting of delirium when possible — can worsen delirium
    - First-generation (typical) antipsychotics have risk for **QT prolongation** — monitor ECG
- **Continued sedation:** may be required once safety is achieved
- Consider physical restraints if an imminent harm to self or others — should be considered a last resort; may also worsen delirium

---

### Nonpharmacologic Techniques

- Frequent reorientation and reassurance
- Listen to the patient — be attentive and receptive
- Be honest and straightforward
- Avoid arguing or condescending
- Respect personal space
- Use concise and simple language
- Offer a chair
- Use of a bedside sitter

---

### Immediate Sedation

**First Generation (Typical) Antipsychotics:**

| Drug | Dose | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Droperidol** | 2.5–5 mg IM/IV | More rapid onset, greater efficacy over haloperidol for acute psychosis/agitation, fewer EPS |
| **Haloperidol (Haldol)** | 2–5 mg IM/IV | |

**Benzodiazepines:**

| Drug | Dose | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Midazolam (Versed)** | 2.5–5 mg IM/IV | More rapid onset but shorter duration than lorazepam |
| **Lorazepam (Ativan)** | 2–4 mg IM/IV | |

!!! tip "Combination Therapy"
    Both antipsychotics and benzodiazepines together achieve more rapid sedation.

**Ketamine:**

- Bolus: **1–2 mg/kg** over 30–60 seconds
- Repeat: **0.5–1.0 mg/kg** after 5–10 minutes if needed

---

### Continued Sedation

**Antipsychotic Medications:**

- Quetiapine (Seroquel)
- Valproic Acid (Depakote)
- Olanzapine (Zyprexa)
- Risperidone (Risperdal)
- Haloperidol (Haldol)

**Continuous IV Sedation:**

- Dexmedetomidine (Precedex)
- Ketamine
