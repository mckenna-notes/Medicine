# IV Contrast AKI

!!! tip "Bottom Line"
    The risk of contrast-associated AKI has been **grossly overstated**.

---

## Provider Note

> It is medically necessary for the patient to receive the requested contrasted scan despite poor kidney function. The benefits outweigh the risks. As appropriate, the patient will be appropriately hydrated and/or treated pre/post imaging with the appropriate labs trended.

---

## ACR / NKF Consensus Statement

**Reference:** Davenport MS, et al. "Use of Intravenous Iodinated Contrast Media in Patients with Kidney Disease: Consensus Statements from the American College of Radiology and the National Kidney Foundation." *Radiology* 294.3 (2020): 660–668.

**Summary:**

The putative risk of administering modern intravenous iodinated contrast media in patients with reduced kidney function has been **overstated** — primarily because of conflation of contrast-**associated** AKI (CA-AKI) with contrast-**induced** AKI (CI-AKI) in uncontrolled studies.

**Key Points:**

- True risk of CI-AKI remains unknown
- **Prophylaxis with IV normal saline** is indicated (without contraindication, e.g. heart failure) for:
    - Patients with **AKI**, or
    - eGFR **<30 mL/min/1.73 m²** not on maintenance dialysis
- Prophylaxis **may be considered** for eGFR 30–44 mL/min/1.73 m² at clinician discretion
- Solitary kidney should **not** independently influence decision making
- Do **not** ad hoc lower contrast dose below diagnostic threshold — risks lowering diagnostic accuracy
- Withhold nephrotoxic medications when feasible in high-risk patients
- Renal replacement therapy should **not** be initiated or altered solely based on contrast administration

## Key Supporting Articles

1. **Aycock RD, Westafer LM, Boxen JL et al.** Acute Kidney Injury after CT: A meta-analysis. *Annals of Emergency Medicine* 2018. (PMID 28811122)
    - Exhaustive, modern literature review showing **no difference** in AKI, mortality, or dialysis with contrast.

2. **Hinson JS, Jalbout NA, Ehmann MR et al.** Acute kidney injury following contrast media administration in the septic patient: A retrospective propensity-matched analysis. *Journal of Critical Care* 2019. (PMID 28131489)
    - Large study at Johns Hopkins in patients with suspected sepsis. **No effect** detected from contrast dye, even among patients with GFR <30 mL/min.

3. **Ehrmann S, Aronson D, Hinson JS.** Contrast-associated acute kidney injury is a myth: Yes. *Intensive Care Medicine* 2017. (PMID 29242967)
